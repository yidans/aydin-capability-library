# Social Science Narrative Style

## Purpose

Use this when drafting or revising a social-science manuscript. The goal is
field-aware argument, not polish.

## Rules

1. **Begin from the phenomenon, not the literature gap.** The Introduction
   names the social, institutional, political, or scientific phenomenon at
   stake. A gap matters only after that stake is clear.

2. **Name the analytical joint.** Do not say the topic is broad or complex.
   State the tension this design can adjudicate: competing mechanisms, an
   unobserved population, measurement boundary, prior-design failure, or
   theory/evidence mismatch.

3. **Write for generalist and specialist.** A specialist needs the frontier; a
   generalist needs terms, acronyms, and datasets glossed on first use. Carry
   both broad stake and precise contribution.

4. **Use concrete scholarly nouns.** Replace "various factors," "different
   aspects," and "broader landscape" with named constructs, populations,
   settings, designs, and outcomes.

5. **Let transitions follow the argument.** Avoid chains of "Furthermore,"
   "Moreover," and "Additionally." Use links that say what changes, or no
   marker when logic is clear.

6. **Make every paragraph earn its place.** Each paragraph needs one job:
   claim, evidence, interpretation, and link to the question. Cut paragraphs
   that only announce, summarize, or repeat the heading.

7. **Calibrate contribution verbs.** Use "shows," "estimates," "documents,"
   "separates," "compares," or "bounds" when supported. Avoid "proves" and
   "solves"; also avoid dissolving a supported result into vague possibility.

8. **Keep rhythm human but scholarly.** Mix short and longer sentences. Avoid
   three consecutive sentences with the same length or opening. Use active voice
   for claims and decisions.

9. **Anchor the stakes with magnitudes.** The Introduction earns attention
   with one or two concrete numbers (the scale of the phenomenon, the share
   affected, the growth rate) — cited, not invented. An intro with zero
   numbers reads as opinion; an intro of raw statistics reads as a table.

10. **When every contrast is null, the descriptive fact is the story.** A
   stable rate ("abandonment has stayed near 30% in AI fields since 2018") is
   a finding a reader keeps; a parade of non-significant contrasts is not.
   Lead with the well-supported descriptive level or trend and report the
   null contrasts as the pre-registered comparisons — honest emphasis, not
   spin.
